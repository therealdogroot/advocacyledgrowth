# Chapter 6: Who creates the content

## Customers, influencers, and employees are not interchangeable

One of the most common mistakes companies make when they start thinking about advocacy is treating all non-branded content as equivalent. A LinkedIn post from a customer, a sponsored post from an influencer, and a thought leadership piece from your VP of Marketing are all "authentic content from a real person." But they are fundamentally different in how buyers perceive them, and that perception determines their value.

## The customer advantage

A verified customer who posts about your product has three things that no other content creator can replicate.

First, they have experiential authority. They use the product every day to do their actual job. When they say "this feature saved our team 10 hours a week," the claim carries weight because the reader knows it comes from lived experience rather than a demo script.

Second, they have peer credibility. If the advocate is a Director of Marketing Operations posting about a marketing tool, other Directors of Marketing Operations pay attention. The reader sees themselves in the advocate: "This person has my job, faces my problems, and found something that works." That identification is enormously powerful, and neither a brand nor an influencer can create it.

Third, they have no obvious agenda. Yes, in an ALG program, advocates receive compensation, and yes, that compensation should be disclosed per FTC guidelines. But the dynamic is different from an influencer deal. The advocate was a customer first. They chose to participate in the program because they already liked the product. The compensation is a thank-you for their time rather than a fee for their opinion, and buyers understand this distinction intuitively.

## Why influencers are different

Professional B2B influencers serve a legitimate purpose. They have audiences, they create well-crafted content, and they can generate awareness quickly. For product launches, event promotion, and top-of-funnel reach, influencer marketing can be effective.

But influencers have a structural limitation when it comes to trust: their business model is creating sponsored content, and everyone knows it. When a B2B influencer with 50,000 LinkedIn followers posts enthusiastically about a product, the sophisticated buyer's first thought is "I wonder how much they got paid for this." The content might be perfectly accurate, but the credibility discount is baked in.

There's also a depth problem. Most B2B influencers are not daily users of the products they promote. They've been briefed, they've seen a demo, maybe they've used the product enough to speak intelligently about it. What they lack is the firsthand operational experience that makes customer content so compelling. They can tell you what the product does. A customer can tell you what it's like to use it on a Tuesday afternoon when three things are going wrong and you need it to work.

## The employee content layer

Employee advocacy, particularly executive thought leadership, is valuable and should be part of any B2B company's content strategy. Your CEO should be posting on LinkedIn. Your product leaders should be sharing what they're building and why. Your sales team should be active and visible.

But employee content, however authentic the individual might be, comes from someone who works for the company. The reader knows the poster has a financial interest in the product succeeding. That doesn't make the content worthless. It does mean the content occupies a different trust tier than content from an external customer.

The smartest companies will run all three: employee thought leadership for brand visibility, selective influencer partnerships for reach, and an advocacy program for trust. They're complementary channels that serve different purposes in the buyer's journey.

But if you had to pick one to invest in first, and you're optimizing for pipeline rather than awareness, advocacy wins. Trust is the scarcest resource in B2B, and customers are the most trusted voice.
